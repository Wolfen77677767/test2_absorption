Theoretical Background

Scientific principles and engineering documentation for Gas Absorption and Stripping calculations.

1. Mass Transfer in Gas–Liquid Systems

Mass transfer between gas and liquid phases occurs when a component migrates across the phase boundary to reach phase equilibrium.

Solute transfer depends directly on the concentration gradient; solute moves from a region of high chemical potential to low potential until balance is achieved.

Industrial examples: CO₂ removal from flue gases, pollution scrubber control, solvent recovery, and natural gas purification operations.

2. Gas Absorption

Gas absorption is the transfer of a soluble component from the gas phase to the contacting liquid phase. Operations are conducted in packed towers or tray columns, maximizing the essential gas-liquid contact area.

The solvent flow rate and gas flow rate govern the removal target.

3. Desorption (Stripping)

Desorption is the reverse of absorption. In this process, the dissolved solute selectively transfers from the liquid volume back out into the gas phase.

It is used fundamentally for solvent regeneration, gas purification, and varying chemical processing cycles.

4. Phase Equilibrium
Equation
y* = m · x

y* = equilibrium mole fraction in the gas phase
x = mole fraction in the liquid phase
m = equilibrium line slope (Henry's law constant)

This mathematical relation physically defines the equilibrium curve utilized in the McCabe-Thiele diagram.

5. Operating Line
Equation
Y = (L/G) X + (Y₀ − (L/G) X₀)

L = liquid flow rate
G = gas flow rate
X₀ = inlet liquid composition
Y₀ = inlet gas composition

The operating line represents the physical mass balance spanning across the structural envelope of the equipment column.

6. Absorption Factor
Equation
A = L / (m · G)

A = absorption factor
If A > 1 → strong absorption
If A < 1 → weak absorption

It drastically affects the total number of stages required for the separation to complete.

7. Stripping Factor
Equation
S = m · G / L

S = stripping factor
If S > 1 → efficient stripping
If S < 1 → weak stripping

8. Cross-Current Stage Model
Equation
x_(i+1) = x_i / (1 + S)

The equation used explicitly in the desorption solver. It illustrates that each isolated stage identically removes a portion of solute from the liquid phase successively.
